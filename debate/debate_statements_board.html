01
Technology

"Smartphones have made loneliness worse, not better."

Case studies

  • Japan's hikikomori — young people who lock themselves in their rooms for years, entirely mediated by screens; estimated 1 million+ affected
  • The iPhone effect — a phone on the table (even face-down) makes conversations measurably shallower, per a University of Essex study
  • Facebook VP's confession — Chamath Palihapitiya publicly said he feels "tremendous guilt" for building tools "ripping apart the social fabric"
  • South Korea — government-funded camps to de-addict teenagers from their phones; treated as a public health crisis
  • Opposite angle — for disabled or isolated people, smartphones are their only social lifeline; loneliness predates the smartphone era

Feed him these

Do you use your phone to avoid eye contact or awkward silences?

Did people used to talk to strangers on trains — and is that actually better?

Is being surrounded by people the same as not being lonely?

Push back: "But you can call your mother in Ukraine anytime now — isn't that worth it?"

02
History

"Germany has dealt with its past better than almost any country — and most nations never do."

Case studies

  • Vergangenheitsbewältigung — Germany has a specific word for "coming to terms with the past"; it's mandatory in every school curriculum
  • Japan vs neighbours — official disputes with China and South Korea persist today over how WWII is taught in Japanese textbooks
  • Russia — Soviet crimes (Gulag, Holodomor, Afghanistan) are largely absent from the official curriculum; actively distorted
  • United States — several states are currently passing laws banning or limiting the teaching of slavery's full history in schools
  • Belgium & Congo — Belgium has never formally acknowledged the Congo genocide under Leopold II; estimated 10 million dead
  • Provocative flip — does obsessive national guilt eventually become performative? Is Germany's identity now defined more by guilt than by anything else?

Feed him these

Living in Germany — does he notice Germans talking about WWII openly, or is it avoided in daily life?

Should statues of controversial historical figures be removed, or kept as reminders?

Does knowing a country's history change how you feel about living there?

Push back: "Ukraine's own history with the Soviet period is complicated — whose job is it to tell that story honestly?"

03
Education

"Most of what schools teach is completely useless in real life."

Case studies

  • Finland's overhaul — replaced traditional subjects with "phenomenon-based learning"; students study climate change across maths, science, and geography simultaneously
  • Peter Thiel's fellowship — pays talented young people $100,000 to drop out of university; argues college is a bubble selling an expensive credential
  • South Korea's exam hell — students study 16 hours a day for one test (Suneung) that determines their entire life; suicide rates spike around exam season
  • Germany's Ausbildung — apprenticeship system is arguably the most successful vocational education model in the world; produces skilled tradespeople without university debt
  • Counterpoint — university graduates consistently earn more and live longer; but is that the education, or just who gets selected into it?

Feed him these

After all those years of medical school — what did he actually learn on the job that school didn't prepare him for?

Should people be able to become doctors through alternative routes — apprenticeships, AI-assisted training?

What subject from school does he actually use? (If he says none — push him to defend that.)

Push back: "But if there were no medical school, would you trust a doctor who hadn't gone through it?"

04
Artificial intelligence

"AI will make doctors unnecessary within 30 years."

Case studies

  • AlphaFold — DeepMind solved protein folding in months; a problem that stumped biologists for 50 years; immediately used in drug discovery
  • Breast cancer detection — AI detected cancer from mammograms with fewer false positives than radiologists, per a 2020 Nature study
  • IDx-DR — first FDA-approved AI that diagnoses diabetic retinopathy without a doctor reviewing the result
  • Babylon Health failure — AI triage app outperformed junior doctors on a licensing exam; also told real patients with serious symptoms they were fine
  • Counterpoint — medicine is not just diagnosis; it's managing fear, communicating death, making ethical trade-offs with a human being in a room

Feed him these

Has he ever had a patient who needed something that wasn't in any textbook — something a machine couldn't give?

If an AI makes a wrong diagnosis and a patient dies — who is responsible? The hospital? The software company?

Would he trust an AI's diagnosis for himself, or would he want a second human opinion?

Push back: "Pilots still exist even though planes can fly themselves — why would doctors be different?"

05
Culture

"Watching a great film is just as intellectually valuable as reading a book."

Case studies

  • Chernobyl (HBO, 2019) — sparked a massive surge in historical reading about the Soviet Union; tourism to the exclusion zone tripled after the series aired
  • The Wire — taught in university sociology and criminology courses as a serious academic text on urban poverty and failing institutions
  • Nicholas Carr's The Shallows — argues screens are literally rewiring our brains to be incapable of reading long-form text; attention spans measurably shorter
  • Reading by country — the average Norwegian reads 47 books a year; the average American reads 4; the average Ukrainian reads more than both; is this cultural or structural?
  • Provocative flip — is someone who has seen every Tarkovsky film more "cultured" than someone who only reads airport thrillers?

Feed him these

What was the last film or series he watched? — don't ask what he liked; ask what he found unrealistic or disagreed with.

Has a film ever made him think differently about something — genuinely changed his mind?

Is audiobooks "reading"? Does the format change what you get from it?

Push back: "But you can't skim a film — you can't go back and re-read a difficult scene the way you can a paragraph."

06
Reading & literature

"People who don't read books are at a serious disadvantage in life — whether they know it or not."

Case studies

  • Theory of mind studies — reading literary fiction specifically improves the ability to understand what other people are feeling; non-readers score measurably lower on empathy tests
  • Warren Buffett — spends roughly 80% of his working day reading; credits it as the single most important habit of his career
  • Cuba — 99.8% literacy rate; one of the most well-read populations in the world despite extreme poverty; reading was a revolutionary political act under Castro
  • The Matthew effect — children who read early become better readers exponentially faster; non-readers fall further behind; the gap is nearly impossible to close after age 10
  • Socrates counterpoint — never wrote anything down; thought writing made people intellectually lazy; argued real knowledge only comes from dialogue

Feed him these

When did he last read a book that wasn't medical — and what was it?

Does he think non-reading doctors are worse doctors — or does clinical experience compensate?

Is there a difference between reading for information and reading for pleasure — and does it matter which one you do?

Push back: "Elon Musk reads constantly — so does Putin. Reading doesn't make you a good person. So what exactly does it give you?"

07
Privacy

"People who say 'I have nothing to hide' don't understand what privacy actually is."

Case studies

  • China's Social Credit System — citizens lose access to trains, flights, and schools based on behavior scores; some cities display the faces of "deadbeats" on public screens
  • Snowden revelations — the NSA was collecting every American's phone metadata, legally, with government approval, in total secrecy for years
  • Cambridge Analytica — harvested Facebook data from 87 million people without consent; used it to micro-target voters in Brexit and the 2016 US election
  • Glenn Greenwald's rebuttal — "Ask people to email you their passwords — they won't, even though they have nothing to hide"
  • Safety trade-off — CCTV in London has helped prevent or solve multiple terrorist attacks; there is a genuine, concrete benefit to surveillance

Feed him these

As a doctor, his patients' data is among the most sensitive that exists — how does he think about who owns it?

Should patient data be available to AI companies if it could save millions of lives — even without individual consent?

Does he feel more surveilled in Germany than in Ukraine, or less?

Push back: "If a government is democratic and trustworthy, does the surveillance argument even apply?"
