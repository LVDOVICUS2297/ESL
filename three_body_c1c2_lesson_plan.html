Warm-up Entry ticket — first impressions 5 min ▾
Teacher prompt
You finished two chapters — give me one word for how you felt at the end of chapter 21. Just one word, then tell me why.
Follow-up if she says something vague like "interesting": Interesting how? Surprising? Disturbing? Exciting? — push for precision.
Goal
Activate engagement and establish emotional entry point before any analysis. Keeps her talking but the one-word constraint reins her in from the start.
Vocabulary 8 words pulled from the text 10 min ▾
Target words — contextual, high-yield
synchronous adj
happening at the same time or speed
"stars in synchronous orbit" — all moving together
solemn adj
serious, formal, full of weight
"solemn power" of the fleet's movement
purge v
to forcibly remove people from a group
Pan Han calls to "purge the Redemptionists"
ratify v
to officially approve a decision
"the decision was ratified by the committee"
zealot / extremist n
someone with intense, inflexible beliefs
Evans's followers described as "madmen who hated the human race"
faction n
a group within a larger group with different goals
Redemptionists vs Adventists within ETO
intercept v
to stop and take something before it reaches its target
Adventists "intercepted messages from our Lord"
tyranny n
cruel, oppressive rule or power
"Eliminate human tyranny!" — Ye Wenjie's rallying cry
Quick exercise — context matching (oral)
Read each gap aloud. Student fills in the word.
1. "The rebels wanted to _____ all members who disagreed with their leader." (purge)
2. "The two sides had become separate _____ — they shared a name but not a goal." (factions)
3. "The vote was taken and the plan was _____ by twelve members." (ratified)
4. "She spoke with a _____ expression — no smiles, no warmth, only resolve." (solemn)
Extension — register stretch
Ask: Can you use "purge" in a non-political sentence — something from everyday life? (e.g. "I purged my wardrobe." / "She purged old files.") — draws attention to register shift between formal/political and casual use.
Comprehension Text check — facts and inference 8 min ▾
Factual recall — short answers only
What does Wang Miao see in the sky at the beginning of chapter 20, and what is it?
Expected: a square formation of stars — the Trisolaran fleet in orbit.
What are the three factions mentioned inside the ETO meeting in chapter 21?
Adventists, Redemptionists, and the Organisation as a whole. If she only names two, probe: "Is there a third group — or a broader body they all belong to?"
What crime does Ye Wenjie accuse the Adventists of committing against the Organisation?
Intercepting and distorting messages from the Trisolarans, and sending unauthorised information.
Inference — one step deeper
Pan Han says he acted out of "duty." Ye Wenjie says he "violated the Organisation's rules." Who is more right — and does it matter?
No right answer. The goal is to get her to distinguish between subjective moral framing and institutional legitimacy. Watch for English structures: "He believes that…" / "From his point of view…" / "However, objectively…"
Discussion 3 discussion threads — pick 2 22 min (~11 min each) ▾
Choose based on energy in the room. Thread A is safest for warmth; Thread C is highest risk / highest reward for a talkative student.
Thread A — The Trisolaran fleet (chapter 20)
The Trisolarans watch their fleet leave, knowing they will never see the outcome. They stand in silence. Why do you think Liu Cixin chose silence here — rather than celebration?
Push for: What is the emotional difference between hope and certainty? Can you hope for something you know will not happen in your lifetime?
If humanity sent a fleet to another star today — knowing no one alive would see it arrive — would that feel meaningful? Would people support it?
Reining-in technique: if she talks too long, ask "Can you sum that up in one sentence?"
Thread B — Ye Wenjie as a character
Ye Wenjie speaks gently, calls people "comrades," and then has a man killed without blinking. Is she a villain? A leader? Both?
Target language: moral complexity, mixed motives. Listen for: "She is not simply good or bad because…" / "What makes her frightening is…"
Rafael gave a kidney from his dead son to his enemy's child — and still became an extremist. What does this tell us about what despair can do to a person?
This is emotionally weighty — read the room. Excellent for C1 expression of empathy and nuance.
Thread C — The ideology debate (most demanding)
The Adventists believe humanity is so broken it deserves destruction. The Redemptionists want humanity to be saved — by alien intervention. Neither trusts humans to fix themselves. Is there anything in the world today that makes you understand — even slightly — where that despair comes from?
Acknowledge it's a heavy question. Strong reining-in potential: "You've made three points — which one do you think is the strongest?"
Liu Cixin gives us a character, Rafael, whose despair came from genuine tragedy and genuine compassion. Does that make the Adventists' ideology more or less disturbing?
Target: can she distinguish between understanding an origin and endorsing a conclusion? Classic C1 critical thinking territory.
Language focus — during discussion
Soften and hedge: encourage "What troubles me about this is…" / "I can see the argument, but…" / "This is uncomfortable, but…"
Restatement drill: if she uses a complex word incorrectly or vaguely, say: "Interesting — can you say that differently, in simpler words?"
Counter-prompt: after any strong opinion, ask "What would someone who disagreed say?"
Close Exit — reflection + preview 5 min ▾
Exit question
Ye Wenjie ends chapter 21 saying "I've only just started." What do you think she means — about the story, and about herself?
Language reflection
Ask her to pick one word from today's vocabulary and use it in a sentence about something outside the book — her week, the news, anything.
Preview prompt (for next session)
As she reads ahead: "Notice how Liu Cixin handles the question of who deserves to survive — and whose side, if anyone's, he seems to be on."
Teacher note
If she ran long on any discussion thread and you didn't reach all vocabulary items — assign the remaining words as written sentences for next session. Do not rush the close.
