Comprehension Understanding check 8 min
Note: These questions test understanding, not memory. The quote gives the student context to locate herself in the text before answering. Click "show answer" only after she has responded.
Chapter 20 · Question 1
Wang Miao is confused by what he sees in the sky. What is it — and what does it tell us about where Trisolaran civilisation has arrived?
A stranger explains to Wang:
"The Trisolaran Interstellar Fleet. It's about to begin its expedition… All those magnificent ships can reach one-tenth the speed of light."
The formation of over a thousand "stars" is actually the Trisolaran fleet in synchronous orbit. After civilisation after civilisation was destroyed by the chaotic three-sun system, Trisolaris has finally achieved interstellar travel — the technology to leave and search for a new world.
→ Ask: What does it feel like to watch something leave that you know you'll never see return? Why might Liu Cixin open Part III with this image?
Chapter 20 · Question 2
The crowd watches the fleet disappear in silence. What are they actually hoping for — and why is it bittersweet?
The narrator describes what this moment means:
"They would not know the outcome of the launch in their lifetimes, but four or five hundred years from now, their descendants would receive the news from a new world."
They're hoping for a future home for Trisolaran civilisation — but no one alive will know if it succeeds. The hope belongs entirely to people not yet born. That's why the silence feels so heavy: it's not celebration, it's sacrifice. They are grieving a future they will never witness.
→ Ask: Is it meaningful to work toward something you'll never see completed? Can you think of real examples — in history, or personally?
Chapter 21 · Question 3
Pan Han justifies killing Shen Yufei as necessary for the Organisation. How does Ye Wenjie respond — and what does her response reveal about her?
Ye addresses Pan Han in front of everyone:
"The ultimate goal and ideal of the ETO is to lose everything. Everything that now belongs to the human race, including us."
Ye doesn't argue emotionally — she reframes the entire conversation. She reminds Pan that the ETO's purpose includes the destruction of humanity itself, including all of them. Her calm is more frightening than anger. She doesn't need to raise her voice — shortly after, she has Pan killed with a single nod. Absolute authority expressed through total stillness.
→ Ask: What kind of leader speaks gently and still terrifies everyone in the room? Is Ye Wenjie a villain, a true believer, or something harder to name?
Chapter 21 · Question 4
Rafael reveals the Adventists' real goal — which is different from what most ETO members believed. What is it, and why does it matter that Evans hid it?
Rafael confesses to the assembly:
"The human race is an evil species… The ultimate goal of the Adventists is to ask our Lord to carry out this divine punishment: the destruction of all humankind."
Most members thought the ETO wanted Trisolaran guidance to improve humanity. The Adventists secretly want humanity destroyed entirely. Evans built the whole organisation on a lie — which means even the people inside the movement never gave informed consent to what they were actually working toward. Everyone was used.
→ Ask: Rafael gave his dead son's kidney to an enemy's child — out of love — and still ended up here. What does that journey tell us about what extreme grief can do to a person?
Chapter 21 · Question 5
Wang Miao has been almost invisible in this meeting. Then Ye Wenjie names him and names his work. What is the effect of that moment?
Ye turns to Wang in front of the entire room:
"This is Professor Wang, a member of the Chinese Academy of Sciences and my friend… This is the first technology our Lord wishes to extinguish from the Earth."
Wang is no longer invisible. Ye has made him complicit in the room's eyes — and told him his life's work is a target. He nearly collapses. The scene is also deeply unsettling because this woman, who just ordered a man killed, is smiling at him warmly and calling him her friend. The warmth is the threat.
→ Ask: Wang grabs someone's sleeve to stay upright — and the man brushes his hand away. What does that tiny detail add? What does it feel like to be invisible to the people around you in a moment of crisis?
Discussion 3 threads — choose 2 22 min (~11 min each)
Selection guide: Thread A (the fleet) is the gentlest entry. Thread B (Ye Wenjie) is reliably engaging. Thread C (ideology) is the most demanding — highest risk, highest reward. Default to A + B; use C if she's sharp and focused that day.
Discussion threads
Thread A The fleet — hope across centuries ▾
The Trisolarans watch their fleet leave in silence — knowing they'll never see the outcome. Liu Cixin chose silence instead of celebration. Why do you think he made that choice?
Push for: What is the difference between hope and certainty? Can you hope for something you know will not happen in your lifetime?
If humanity sent a fleet to another star today — knowing no one alive would see it arrive — would that feel meaningful? Would people support it?
Reining-in move: if she speaks for too long, ask "Can you summarise that in one sentence?"
Thread B Ye Wenjie — the gentlest killer ▾
Ye Wenjie speaks gently, calls people "comrades," and then has a man killed without blinking. Is she a villain? A leader? Both? Neither?
Target language: moral complexity, mixed motives. Listen for: "She is not simply good or bad because…" / "What makes her frightening is…"
Rafael donated his dead son's kidney to his enemy's child — out of genuine compassion — and still became an extremist. What does that arc tell us about despair?
Read the room on this one — it is emotionally weighty. Strong for C1 expression of empathy and nuance.
Thread C The ideology — when despair becomes doctrine ▾
The Adventists believe humanity is so broken it deserves destruction. The Redemptionists want humanity saved — by alien force. Neither trusts humans to fix themselves. Is there anything in the world today that makes you even slightly understand where that despair comes from?
Acknowledge it's heavy. Reining-in move: "You've made three points — which do you think is the strongest?"
Liu Cixin gives us Rafael — a man whose despair came from genuine love and genuine tragedy. Does that make the Adventists' ideology more disturbing or less?
Target: can she distinguish between understanding an origin and endorsing a conclusion? Core C1 critical thinking.
Language focus — active during discussion
Hedging: encourage "What troubles me about this is…" / "I can see the argument, but…" / "This is uncomfortable to say, but…"
Restatement drill: if she uses a word vaguely — say "Interesting. Can you say that differently, in simpler words?"
Counter-prompt: after any strong opinion, ask "What would someone who disagreed say?"
