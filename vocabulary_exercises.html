Exercise 1

Word Families

Study the table below. Notice which forms exist and which don't — not every root has all four forms. Pay attention to the meaning column before moving on.

Root / Verb Noun Adjective Adverb Core meaning
acquit acquittal acquitted — To formally declare someone not guilty of a crime
brazen — brazen brazenly Bold in a shocking or shameless way; showing no guilt
decorum decorum decorous decorously Behaviour that is correct and appropriate for the situation
eccentric eccentricity eccentric eccentrically Unusual or unconventional in behaviour; odd in a distinctive way
felicitous felicity felicitous felicitously Well-chosen and pleasing; apt and fortunate (formal / literary)
maneuver / manoeuvre maneuver maneuverable — To move or guide skilfully, often through a complex situation
premonition premonition premonitory — A strong feeling that something (usually bad) is about to happen
inexorable inexorability inexorable inexorably Impossible to stop or prevent; relentlessly continuing

💡 Notice: brazenly and inexorably are sentence adverbials — they colour the entire action, not just the verb. Felicitous and decorous are formal/literary register; you're unlikely to hear them in casual speech.

Exercise 2

Collocations: Same or Different?

First, study the reference table. Then read each pair of sentences (A and B). They use the same collocation — but is it used correctly in both, only one, or neither? Choose your answer and be ready to explain why.

Collocation Reference
Collocation Meaning Example
strengthen bonds to make relationships or connections deeper and more solid Regular team lunches help strengthen bonds between colleagues.
exacerbate divisions to make existing disagreements or splits worse The new policy only exacerbated divisions within the group.
engender distrust to cause or produce a feeling of distrust (formal) His evasive answers engendered distrust among the committee.
aggravate the problem to make a problem more serious or severe Ignoring complaints will only aggravate the problem.
ease stress levels to reduce or relieve stress gradually Short breaks during the day can ease stress levels considerably.
boost morale to increase people's confidence, enthusiasm, or sense of purpose Public recognition of good work boosts morale across the whole team.
alleviate boredom to partially reduce or relieve feelings of boredom She rearranged her desk to alleviate the boredom of the long afternoon.
  • strengthen bonds
    A. The new shared project was intended to strengthen bonds between the two departments that had long worked in isolation.
    B. After the argument, she sent flowers hoping it would strengthen the bond she had broken with her colleague.
  • exacerbate divisions
    A. The manager's favouritism exacerbated divisions in a team that was already struggling to communicate.
    B. The mediator's calm approach managed to exacerbate the divisions and bring both sides closer.
  • engender distrust
    A. Transparency in decision-making is crucial — secrecy will inevitably engender distrust.
    B. The warm welcome and open communication began to engender distrust among the new staff members.
  • aggravate the problem
    A. Sending a passive-aggressive email at midnight will only aggravate the problem rather than resolve it.
    B. The technician's quick fix aggravated the problem and the system was fully restored within minutes.
  • ease stress levels
    A. The company introduced meditation sessions to ease stress levels during the restructuring period.
    B. Doubling the workload during peak season is a proven way to ease stress levels among employees.
  • boost morale
    A. A handwritten thank-you note from the director did more to boost morale than any of the formal appraisal meetings.
    B. The sudden announcement of layoffs was intended to boost morale and motivate the remaining staff.
  • alleviate boredom
    A. She started a podcast during lockdown purely to alleviate the boredom of empty evenings at home.
    B. The repetitive, unchallenging tasks did nothing to alleviate boredom — they made it considerably worse.

💡 Discussion prompt: For any pair where you chose "only A" or "only B" — what exactly is wrong with the other sentence? Is it the meaning of the verb, the logic of the context, or the connotation?

Exercise 4

Writing Task: No Notes Allowed

Read the scenario. Write 120–180 words responding to it. Use at least 5 of the 8 single words from Exercise 1 and at least 3 of the 7 collocations from Exercise 2. No reference materials — work from memory.

Scenario

It is the third hour of a mandatory all-staff video call. The connection has been unstable all morning. One participant — a senior figure known for unconventional behaviour — has been speaking over others, ignoring the agenda, and at one point seemed to be having a separate phone conversation. The meeting organiser is struggling to maintain control. You are observing and later write a short account of what happened and how different people responded.

  • Use at least 5 of the 8 single words (acquitted, brazenly, decorum, eccentric, felicitous, inexorably, maneuvered, premonition)
  • Use at least 3 of the 7 collocations (strengthen bonds, exacerbate divisions, engender distrust, aggravate the problem, ease stress levels, boost morale, alleviate boredom)
  • Write in the past tense — this is a retrospective account
  • Aim for 120–180 words

💡 After writing, swap with a partner (or share your screen) and check: did every word feel natural in context, or does any use feel forced? That instinct — noticing when a word feels "bolted on" — is exactly what distinguishes B2 from C1.
